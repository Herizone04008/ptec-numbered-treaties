Historic Terms of Treaty 1

Overview of Treaty 1 Terms

Treaty 1 included several foundational agreements between the Crown and the Anishinaabe and Dakota Nations, shaping how both groups would share the land and live alongside one another. The treaty outlined the government’s responsibility to provide support such as education, tools, and annual payments, while also recognizing that Indigenous Nations would continue to maintain their own identities, cultures, and inherent rights. These terms were meant to create a relationship based on cooperation and mutual respect, where settlers could establish communities while Indigenous peoples retained control over their ways of life.

Land Allotments

Each Indigenous family of five was promised 160 acres of land. This land was meant to support farming, community development, and long-term sustainability. However, in many cases, the land provided was smaller than promised or located in areas that were difficult to farm. Additionally, the process of allotting land to individual families often disrupted traditional communal living arrangements and cultural practices. The Crown’s failure to fully honor these land promises has had lasting impacts on Indigenous communities, contributing to economic hardships and undermining the trust that was supposed to be the foundation of the treaty relationship.

Outside Promises

In addition to the written treaty, several outside promises were made during negotiations. These included:

  • Clothing for Chiefs and leaders
  • Tools and farming equipment
  • Support for education
  • Annual payments (annuities)

Many of these promises were not written into the official treaty text but were verbally agreed upon during negotiations. Indigenous leaders understood these commitments as integral parts of the agreement, and they expected them to be fulfilled alongside the land terms. However, over time, many of these outside promises were not fully honored, leading to feelings of betrayal and mistrust. The failure to uphold these commitments has had lasting impacts on Indigenous communities, contributing to economic hardships and undermining the trust that was supposed to be the foundation of the treaty relationship.

Rights and Responsibilities

Treaty 1 created a framework in which both the Crown and the Indigenous Nations carried important responsibilities meant to guide how they would live together on the land. The Crown committed to providing long‑term support through education, tools, protection, and annual payments, presenting these promises as part of a respectful partnership. In return, the Anishinaabe and Dakota Nations agreed to share portions of their territory so that settlers could establish communities, while still maintaining their own systems of leadership, cultural practices, and community structures. A key principle of the treaty was that Indigenous peoples did not surrender their identity, culture, or inherent rights; instead, they entered the agreement with the understanding that these rights would continue to be recognized and protected. Over time, many of the Crown’s responsibilities were not fully upheld, which created tensions and misunderstandings that still influence Treaty relationships and discussions about reconciliation today.

What Makes Treaty 1 Different?

Treaty 1 stands out as a historically significant agreement because it was one of the earliest numbered treaties negotiated in Western Canada, setting the tone for how future treaties would be approached. Its signing in 1871 marked the beginning of a new era of relationships between the Crown and Indigenous Nations, and many of the structures, expectations, and misunderstandings that appeared in Treaty 1 later repeated across the Prairies. What makes this treaty especially unique is the presence of several outside promises—verbal commitments made during negotiations that were not included in the written text but were still understood by Indigenous leaders as part of the agreement. The involvement of multiple Nations, the early timing of the treaty, and the long‑term consequences of unfulfilled promises all contribute to its importance. Today, Treaty 1 continues to influence discussions about land, rights, and reconciliation, reminding Canadians that the treaty relationship is ongoing and still carries responsibilities for both sides.
