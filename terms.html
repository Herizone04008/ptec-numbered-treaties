Historic Terms of Treaty 1

Overview of Treaty 1 Terms

Treaty 1 included several important agreements between the Crown and the Anishinaabe and Dakota Nations. These terms outlined how land would be shared, what support the government would provide, and what rights Indigenous peoples would maintain. Many of these promises continue to influence Treaty relationships today.

Land Allotments

Each Indigenous family of five was promised 160 acres of land. This land was meant to support farming, community development, and long-term sustainability. However, in many cases, the land provided was smaller than promised or located in areas that were difficult to farm.

Outside Promises

In addition to the written treaty, several outside promises were made during negotiations. These included:

  • Clothing for Chiefs and leaders
  • Tools and farming equipment
  • Support for education
  • Annual payments (annuities)

Many of these promises were not written into the official treaty text but were verbally agreed upon during negotiations.

Rights and Responsibilities

Treaty 1 established a relationship of shared responsibility. The Crown promised to provide support, resources, and protection, while Indigenous Nations agreed to share the land peacefully and allow settlement. Importantly, Indigenous peoples did not surrender their identity, culture, or inherent rights.

What Makes Treaty 1 Different?

Treaty 1 is unique because it was one of the first numbered treaties in Western Canada. It set the foundation for how future treaties would be negotiated. The presence of outside promises, the early date of signing, and the involvement of multiple Nations make Treaty 1 historically significant.
