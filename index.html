Treaty 1 – The Stone Fort Treaty

What is a Treaty?

A treaty is a formal agreement between two or more parties. In Canada, treaties are formal agreements between Indigenous peoples and the Crown that define rights, obligations, and relationships regarding land, resources, and governance.

Treaties are important because they establish the legal and political framework for how Indigenous peoples and the government interact. They often include promises of land, resources, and support in exchange for peaceful coexistence and cooperation.

What is Treaty 1?

Treaty 1, often called the Stone Fort Treaty, is one of the first modern treaties in Canada and was signed on August 3, 1871. It was made to create an agreement between the Canadian government and Indigenous peoples in southern Manitoba so that settlers could move into the area while still protecting Indigenous rights. The treaty is important because it set a pattern for how future agreements between the government and Indigenous nations would be made, making sure there was at least a formal understanding of land sharing, resources, and promises from both sides.

Where is Treaty 1 Located & Negotiated?

Treaty 1 is located in southern Manitoba, Canada. It was negotiated between the Canadian government and the Anishinaabe and Dakota peoples. The negotiations took place at Fort Garry, which is now part of Winnipeg, Manitoba.

The Significance of Treaty 1

Treaty 1 is significant because it set the foundation for how future treaties across Canada would be negotiated. It marked the beginning of formal land agreements in Manitoba and continues to shape relationships, responsibilities, and land rights today. Its impact is still felt in modern governance, community development, and reconciliation efforts.
